🔋 Current Electricity

Understanding the fundamentals of electric current, potential difference, EMF, and resistance

1

Electric Current

📖 Definition of Electric Current

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

In metallic conductors, current is due to the flow of free electrons (negatively charged particles).

📐 Formula

I = Q / t

I = Current (in Amperes, A)

Q = Charge (in Coulombs, C)

t = Time (in seconds, s)

📏 SI Unit

The SI unit of current is the Ampere (A)

1 Ampere = 1 Coulomb per second (1 A = 1 C/s)

This means if 1 coulomb of charge flows past a point in 1 second, the current is 1 ampere.

🔄 Conventional Current vs Electron Flow

Conventional Current

Flows from positive (+) to negative (-) terminal

This is the direction we use in circuit analysis

Electron Flow

Flows from negative (-) to positive (+) terminal

This is the actual direction electrons move

✏️ Worked Example 1

Question: A charge of 180 C flows through a lamp in 1 minute. Calculate the current through the lamp.

Given:

  • Q = 180 C
  • t = 1 minute = 60 s

Using formula: I = Q / t

Calculation:

I = 180 / 60 = 3 A

Answer: The current through the lamp is 3 A

✏️ Worked Example 2

Question: A current of 0.5 A flows through a resistor for 2 minutes. How much charge passes through the resistor?

Given:

  • I = 0.5 A
  • t = 2 minutes = 120 s

Rearranging formula: Q = I × t

Calculation:

Q = 0.5 × 120 = 60 C

Answer: The charge that passes through the resistor is 60 C

Potential Difference (P.D.)

📖 Definition of Potential Difference

Potential difference (p.d.) between two points is the work done per unit charge in moving a charge from one point to another.

It is also known as voltage and represents the "electrical pressure" that pushes charges through a circuit.

📐 Formula

V = W / Q

V = Potential Difference (in Volts, V)

W = Work Done / Energy Transferred (in Joules, J)

Q = Charge (in Coulombs, C)

📏 SI Unit

The SI unit of potential difference is the Volt (V)

1 Volt = 1 Joule per Coulomb (1 V = 1 J/C)

This means if 1 joule of energy is needed to move 1 coulomb of charge between two points, the p.d. is 1 volt.

🔌 Measuring Potential Difference

A voltmeter is used to measure the potential difference between two points.

  • Connected in parallel across the component
  • Has very high resistance so very little current flows through it
  • Does not significantly affect the circuit

📊 How to Connect a Voltmeter

The voltmeter is connected in parallel across the component to measure the p.d. across it.

✏️ Worked Example 3

Question: 60 J of energy is used to move 12 C of charge through a resistor. What is the potential difference across the resistor?

Given:

  • W = 60 J
  • Q = 12 C

Using formula: V = W / Q

Calculation:

V = 60 / 12 = 5 V

Answer: The potential difference across the resistor is 5 V

Electromotive Force (E.M.F.)

📖 Definition of E.M.F.

Electromotive force (e.m.f.) of a source is the work done by the source in driving a unit charge around a complete circuit.

It can also be defined as the energy converted from other forms to electrical energy per unit charge.

📐 Formula

ε = W / Q

ε (epsilon) = E.M.F. (in Volts, V)

W = Work Done / Energy Converted (in Joules, J)

Q = Charge (in Coulombs, C)

⚡ E.M.F. vs Potential Difference

Associated with the source (battery, generator) Associated with components (resistors, bulbs)

Converts other forms of energy to electrical Converts electrical energy to other forms
Measured when no current flows (open circuit) Measured when current flows through circuit

🔗 Cells in Series

When cells are connected in series (positive to negative), the total EMF is the sum of individual EMFs:

εtotal = ε₁ + ε₂ + ε₃ + ...

✏️ Worked Example 4

Question: A battery converts 540 J of chemical energy to electrical energy when 45 C of charge passes through it. Calculate the e.m.f. of the battery.

Given:

  • W = 540 J (energy converted)
  • Q = 45 C

Using formula: ε = W / Q

Calculation:

ε = 540 / 45 = 12 V

Answer: The e.m.f. of the battery is 12 V

📌 Key Points to Remember

  • E.M.F. is the work done per unit charge by the source
  • E.M.F. is measured in Volts (V), same as P.D.
  • E.M.F. is NOT a force - it's energy per unit charge
  • Sources convert other forms of energy to electrical energy
  • For cells in series: Total EMF = sum of individual EMFs
  • E.M.F. is measured across the terminals when no current flows

Resistance & Ohm's Law

📖 Definition of Resistance

Resistance is the opposition to the flow of electric current in a conductor.

It is defined as the ratio of potential difference across a conductor to the current flowing through it.

📐 Ohm's Law Formula

R = V / I

R = Resistance (in Ohms, Ω)

V = Potential Difference (in Volts, V)

I = Current (in Amperes, A)

🔺 The VIR Triangle

Cover the quantity you want to find!

V = I × R I = V / R R = V / I

📏 SI Unit

The SI unit of resistance is the Ohm (Ω)

1 Ohm = 1 Volt per Ampere (1 Ω = 1 V/A)

A conductor has a resistance of 1 ohm if a p.d. of 1 volt causes a current of 1 ampere to flow through it.

⚡ Ohm's Law Statement

Ohm's Law: The current flowing through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it, provided the temperature and other physical conditions remain constant.

I ∝ V (at constant temperature)

📊 Ohmic vs Non-Ohmic Conductors

Ohmic Conductors
  • Obey Ohm's Law
  • Constant resistance
  • I-V graph is a straight line through origin
  • Example: Metal wire at constant temperature
Non-Ohmic Conductors
  • Do NOT obey Ohm's Law
  • Resistance changes with current/voltage
  • I-V graph is NOT a straight line
  • Examples: Filament lamp, diode, thermistor, LDR

✏️ Worked Example 5

Question: A resistor has a resistance of 20 Ω. If a current of 0.3 A flows through it, calculate the potential difference across the resistor.

Given:

  • R = 20 Ω
  • I = 0.3 A

Using formula: V = I × R

Calculation:

V = 0.3 × 20 = 6 V

Answer: The potential difference across the resistor is 6 V

✏️ Worked Example 6

Question: The p.d. across a lamp is 12 V and the current through it is 2.5 A. What is the resistance of the lamp?

Given:

  • V = 12 V
  • I = 2.5 A

Using formula: R = V / I

Calculation:

R = 12 / 2.5 = 4.8 Ω

Answer: The resistance of the lamp is 4.8 Ω

  • SI unit of resistance is Ohm (Ω)

Non-Ohmic Conductors

🔍 What are Non-Ohmic Conductors?

Non-ohmic conductors are components whose resistance changes depending on conditions like:

  • The current flowing through them
  • The voltage across them
  • Temperature
  • Light intensity

Their I-V graphs are NOT straight lines, showing that they do NOT obey Ohm's Law.

💡

Filament Lamp

Resistance increases with temperature

Circuit Symbol (Singapore O-Level)

🔬 How It Works

  1. When current flows, the filament heats up
  2. Higher temperature causes metal atoms to vibrate more
  3. Electrons collide more frequently with vibrating atoms
  4. This increases the resistance
  5. As V increases, I increases but at a decreasing rate

Semiconductor Diode

Allows current in one direction only

Circuit Symbol

Forward Bias (+V)

  • Very low resistance
  • Diode is "ON"

  • Positive connected to cathode
  • Almost no current flows
  • Very high resistance
  • Diode is "OFF"

NTC = Negative Temperature Coefficient

  4. More charge carriers available → lower resistance
  5. Opposite behavior to metals!

🔌 Direct Current (DC) Circuits

Series Circuits

📖 What is a Series Circuit?

A series circuit is one in which components are connected end-to-end, forming a single path for current to flow.

If any component breaks or is disconnected, the entire circuit stops working.

📐 Series Circuit Diagram

📏 Key Rules for Series Circuits

🔵
Rule 1: Current is the SAME everywhere
I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃ = ...

The same current flows through all components because there is only ONE path.

🟡
Rule 2: Voltages ADD UP
Vtotal = V₁ + V₂ + V₃ + ...

The sum of p.d. across all components equals the EMF of the supply.

🟠
Rule 3: Resistances ADD UP
Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ + ...

Total resistance is the sum of all individual resistances.

✏️ Worked Example: Series Circuit

Question: Two resistors of 15 Ω and 25 Ω are connected in series to a 12 V battery. Calculate:

  1. The total resistance
  2. The current in the circuit
  3. The p.d. across each resistor

Given: R₁ = 15 Ω, R₂ = 25 Ω, EMF = 12 V

(a) Total Resistance:

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ = 15 + 25 = 40 Ω

(b) Current:

I = V / R = 12 / 40 = 0.3 A

(c) P.D. across each resistor:

V₁ = I × R₁ = 0.3 × 15 = 4.5 V

V₂ = I × R₂ = 0.3 × 25 = 7.5 V

Check: V₁ + V₂ = 4.5 + 7.5 = 12 V = EMF ✓

⚖️ Advantages & Disadvantages of Series Circuits

✅ Advantages
  • Simple to build and understand
  • Adding resistors increases total resistance
  • Easy to add protective components (fuses)
❌ Disadvantages
  • If one component fails, entire circuit breaks
  • Adding more components reduces current
  • Each component gets only a share of voltage

📌 Key Points: Series Circuits

  • Components connected end-to-end in a single path
  • Current is the same through all components
  • Voltages add up to equal the EMF
  • Resistances add up: Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + ...
  • Breaking one component breaks the whole circuit
  • Used in: Christmas lights (old type), voltage dividers
2

Parallel Circuits

📖 What is a Parallel Circuit?

A parallel circuit is one in which components are connected across each other, providing multiple paths for current to flow.

If one component fails, the others continue to work because current can flow through other paths.

📐 Parallel Circuit Diagram

📏 Key Rules for Parallel Circuits

🟡
Rule 1: Voltage is the SAME across each branch
V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃ = ...

All components in parallel have the same p.d. across them.

🔵
Rule 2: Currents ADD UP
Itotal = I₁ + I₂ + I₃ + ...

Total current from supply equals sum of currents in each branch.

🟠
Rule 3: Reciprocals of Resistance ADD UP
1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃ + ...

Total resistance is LESS than the smallest individual resistance!

📐 Special Formula: Two Resistors in Parallel

For exactly TWO resistors in parallel, use this shortcut:

Rtotal = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂)

Remember: "Product over Sum"

💡 Current Distribution Rule

In parallel circuits, more current flows through the path of lower resistance.

Think of it like water: water prefers to flow through wider pipes (less resistance)!

✏️ Worked Example: Parallel Circuit

Question: Two resistors of 6 Ω and 12 Ω are connected in parallel to a 12 V supply. Calculate:

  1. The total resistance
  2. The current through each resistor
  3. The total current from the supply

Given: R₁ = 6 Ω, R₂ = 12 Ω, V = 12 V

(a) Total Resistance:

Rtotal = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂)

Rtotal = (6 × 12) / (6 + 12) = 72 / 18 = 4 Ω

(b) Current through each resistor:

I₁ = V / R₁ = 12 / 6 = 2 A

I₂ = V / R₂ = 12 / 12 = 1 A

(c) Total current:

Itotal = I₁ + I₂ = 2 + 1 = 3 A

Check: Itotal = V / Rtotal = 12 / 4 = 3 A ✓

Note: More current (2 A) flows through the smaller resistor (6 Ω) than through the larger one (1 A through 12 Ω).

⚖️ Advantages & Disadvantages of Parallel Circuits

✅ Advantages
  • If one component fails, others continue working
  • Each component gets full supply voltage
  • Easy to add or remove components
  • Components can be controlled independently
❌ Disadvantages
  • Uses more wiring
  • Adding more components increases total current
  • May require higher capacity power supply

🏠 Real World: Household Wiring

All electrical appliances in your home are connected in parallel!

💡

Each light can be switched on/off independently

🔌

All outlets provide the same voltage (230V in Singapore)

📺

Unplugging one appliance doesn't affect others

📌 Key Points: Parallel Circuits

  • Components connected across each other, multiple paths
  • Voltage is the same across all branches
  • Currents add up: Itotal = I₁ + I₂ + ...
  • Reciprocals of resistance add up: 1/Rtotal = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...
  • Total resistance is LESS than smallest individual resistance
  • More current flows through lower resistance path
  • Used in: Household wiring, car electrical systems

Many practical circuits contain both series and parallel combinations of components.

To analyze these circuits, we need to:

  1. Identify which components are in series and which are in parallel
  2. Simplify the circuit step by step
  3. Calculate total resistance first
  4. Then find currents and voltages

✏️ Worked Example: Combined Circuit

Question: In the circuit below, R₁ = 4 Ω is in series with a parallel combination of R₂ = 6 Ω and R₃ = 12 Ω. The battery has an EMF of 10 V. Calculate:

  1. The total resistance of the circuit
  2. The current from the battery
  3. The p.d. across R₁
  4. The current through R₂ and R₃

Given: R₁ = 4 Ω, R₂ = 6 Ω, R₃ = 12 Ω, EMF = 10 V

(a) Total Resistance:

First, find R₂||R₃ = (6 × 12) / (6 + 12) = 72/18 = 4 Ω

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂||R₃ = 4 + 4 = 8 Ω

(b) Current from battery:

I = EMF / Rtotal = 10 / 8 = 1.25 A

(c) P.D. across R₁:

V₁ = I × R₁ = 1.25 × 4 = 5 V

(d) Current through R₂ and R₃:

P.D. across parallel part = EMF - V₁ = 10 - 5 = 5 V

I₂ = V / R₂ = 5 / 6 = 0.833 A

I₃ = V / R₃ = 5 / 12 = 0.417 A

Check: I₂ + I₃ = 0.833 + 0.417 = 1.25 A = Total current ✓

📌 Key Points: Combined Circuits

  • Identify series and parallel parts first
  • Simplify parallel parts using Rparallel = (R₁ × R₂) / (R₁ + R₂)
  • Add series resistances: Rtotal = R1 + Rparallel + ...
  • Current through series parts is the same
  • Voltage across parallel parts is the same
  • Always verify your answer using Kirchhoff's laws

Potential Divider

⭐ Important Concept

📖 What is a Potential Divider?

A potential divider (or voltage divider) is a circuit that uses two or more resistors in series to divide a voltage into smaller parts.

It allows us to obtain a fraction of the input voltage as output, which is very useful in electronics and sensor circuits.

⭐ Why is this Important?

  • Used in sensor circuits with thermistors and LDRs
  • Essential for voltage regulation
  • Found in volume controls and dimmers
  • Commonly tested in O-Level examinations

📐 Potential Divider Circuit

📐 The Potential Divider Formula

Vout = Vin × (R₂ / (R₁ + R₂))
Vout = Output voltage (voltage across R₂)
Vin = Input voltage (total EMF)
R₁ = First resistor (top)
R₂ = Second resistor (bottom) - output is taken across this

Alternative form: The voltage across R₁ is:

VR1 = Vin × (R₁ / (R₁ + R₂))

🧠 Understanding the Formula

1️⃣
Current through both resistors is the same

I = Vin / (R₁ + R₂)

2️⃣
Voltage across R₂ (using V = IR)

Vout = I × R₂ = [Vin / (R₁ + R₂)] × R₂

3️⃣
Simplify to get the formula

Vout = Vin × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)

💡 Key Insight

The larger the resistance, the larger the share of voltage it gets.

Voltage is divided in the ratio of resistances:

V₁ : V₂ = R₁ : R₂

🎯 Design Challenge

Challenge 1:

Design a potential divider to give an output of 3V from a 9V supply.

Vout/Vin = R₂/(R₁ + R₂)

3/9 = 1/3, so R₂ = (1/3) × (R₁ + R₂)

This means R₂ : R₁ = 1 : 2

Example: R₁ = 20 kΩ, R₂ = 10 kΩ

Challenge 2:

You have a 12V supply and want an output of 8V. If R₁ = 10 kΩ, what should R₂ be?

8 = 12 × R₂ / (10 + R₂)

8(10 + R₂) = 12R₂

80 + 8R₂ = 12R₂

80 = 4R₂

R₂ = 20 kΩ

✏️ Worked Example 1: Basic Potential Divider

Question: A potential divider consists of a 15 kΩ resistor (R₁) and a 5 kΩ resistor (R₂) connected in series to a 6V supply. Calculate the output voltage across R₂.

Given: R₁ = 15 kΩ, R₂ = 5 kΩ, Vin = 6 V

Using potential divider formula:

Vout = Vin × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)

Vout = 6 × 5 / (15 + 5)

Vout = 6 × 5 / 20

Vout = 6 × 0.25 = 1.5 V

Answer: The output voltage is 1.5 V

✏️ Worked Example 2: Thermistor Potential Divider

Question: A thermistor with resistance 8 kΩ at room temperature is connected in series with a 12 kΩ fixed resistor to form a potential divider with a 5V supply. The thermistor is R₂ (bottom resistor).

  1. Calculate the output voltage at room temperature.
  2. If the temperature increases and the thermistor resistance drops to 2 kΩ, what is the new output voltage?
  3. Explain how this circuit could be used as a temperature warning system.

Given: R₁ = 12 kΩ (fixed), R₂ = thermistor, Vin = 5 V

(a) At room temperature (R₂ = 8 kΩ):

Vout = 5 × 8 / (12 + 8) = 5 × 8 / 20 = 2.0 V

(b) At higher temperature (R₂ = 2 kΩ):

Vout = 5 × 2 / (12 + 2) = 5 × 2 / 14 = 0.71 V

(c) Temperature warning application:

  • Connect Vout to a processor or comparator circuit
  • When temperature rises, Vout decreases (from 2.0V to 0.71V)
  • Set a threshold voltage (e.g., 1.0V)
  • When Vout drops below threshold, trigger an alarm or activate cooling

📌 Key Points: Potential Divider (IMPORTANT!)

  • Formula: Vout = Vin × R₂ / (R₁ + R₂)
  • Voltage divides in the ratio of resistances: V₁ : V₂ = R₁ : R₂

Electrical Power (P = VI)

📖 Definition of Electrical Power

Electrical power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted to other forms of energy.

It tells us how quickly an appliance uses electrical energy.

📐 The Power Formula

P = V × I
P = Power (in Watts, W)
V = Potential Difference (in Volts, V)
I = Current (in Amperes, A)

📏 SI Unit of Power

The SI unit of power is the Watt (W)

1 Watt = 1 Joule per second (1 W = 1 J/s)

1 Watt = 1 Volt × 1 Ampere (1 W = 1 V × 1 A)

Common Power Units:
1 kW = 1000 W
1 MW = 1,000,000 W

📐 Derived Power Formulas

By combining P = VI with Ohm's Law (V = IR), we get two more useful formulas:

P = VI but V = IR ∴ P = (IR) × I
P = I²R

Use when you know current and resistance

P = VI but I = V/R ∴ P = V × (V/R)
P = V²/R

Use when you know voltage and resistance

✏️ Worked Example 1: Finding Current

Question: An electric kettle has a power rating of 2300 W. It operates at the mains voltage of 230 V in Singapore. Calculate the current flowing through the kettle.

Given: P = 2300 W, V = 230 V

Find: I = ?

Using formula: P = VI

Rearranging: I = P / V

Calculation:

I = 2300 / 230 = 10 A

Answer: The current flowing through the kettle is 10 A

💡 Note: This is why kettles need a high-rated fuse (13 A) and thicker cables!

✏️ Worked Example 2: Finding Resistance

Question: A 60 W light bulb operates at 230 V. Calculate:

  1. The current through the bulb
  2. The resistance of the bulb filament

Given: P = 60 W, V = 230 V

(a) Current:

I = P / V = 60 / 230 = 0.26 A

(b) Resistance (Method 1):

R = V / I = 230 / 0.26 = 885 Ω

OR Resistance (Method 2):

R = V² / P = 230² / 60 = 52900 / 60 = 882 Ω

(Small difference due to rounding)

Electrical Energy (E = Pt)

📖 Definition of Electrical Energy

Electrical energy is the energy transferred or converted by an electrical appliance.

It depends on both the power of the appliance and the time it is used.

📐 The Energy Formula

E = P × t
E = Energy (in Joules, J)
P = Power (in Watts, W)
t = Time (in seconds, s)

📐 Derived Energy Formulas

By substituting P = VI, we get:

E = V × I × t

Energy in terms of voltage, current, and time

E = I²Rt

Energy in terms of current, resistance, and time

E = V²t/R

Energy in terms of voltage, resistance, and time

⚡ The Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

While the SI unit of energy is the Joule, electricity companies use the kilowatt-hour (kWh) for billing.

1 kWh = 1 kW × 1 hour
Converting kWh to Joules:

1 kWh = 1000 W × 3600 s = 3,600,000 J = 3.6 MJ

💡 1 kWh is also called 1 "unit" of electricity

Dangers of Household Electricity

⚠️ Electricity Can Be Dangerous!

Household electricity at 230V AC in Singapore is potentially lethal. Understanding the dangers helps us stay safe.

⚡

Electric Shock

What happens: Electric current passes through the human body

Effects depend on current:
1 mA Tingling sensation
10-20 mA Muscle contraction, cannot let go
50-100 mA Heart fibrillation, potentially fatal
> 100 mA Severe burns, cardiac arrest
Common causes:
  • Touching live wires
  • Using faulty appliances
  • Water contact with electricity
  • Damaged insulation
🔥

Electrical Fire

What happens: Excessive heat from high current or poor connections ignites nearby materials

Common causes:
🔌
Overloading

Too many appliances on one socket

⚡
Short Circuit

Live and neutral wires touch directly

🔗
Loose Connections

Poor contact causes sparking

🪫
Damaged Cables

Worn insulation exposes wires

🎮 Interactive: Understanding Electrical Dangers
Select a scenario to learn about:
⚡ Short Circuit

A short circuit occurs when the live and neutral wires come into direct contact, bypassing the appliance.

  • Resistance becomes very low (almost zero)
  • Very large current flows (I = V/R, with small R)
  • Generates extreme heat quickly
  • Can cause fire or explosion

If R → 0, then I → ∞ (very large current!)

🔌 Overloading

Overloading occurs when too many appliances draw current from one socket.

  • Total current exceeds cable rating
  • Heating effect: Heat = I²Rt
  • Cables overheat and insulation melts
  • Can cause fire

Total current = Sum of all appliance currents

🔗 Damaged Insulation

Damaged insulation exposes live wires, creating shock and fire hazards.

  • People can accidentally touch live wires
  • Exposed wires can touch each other (short circuit)
  • Can touch metal parts of appliances

Always check cables regularly and replace damaged ones!

💧 Water and Electricity

Water conducts electricity, especially tap water which contains dissolved salts.

  • Water creates a path for current to flow through your body
  • Wet skin has much lower resistance than dry skin
  • Bathrooms and kitchens are high-risk areas

Never use electrical appliances with wet hands or near water!

🔥 The Heating Effect of Current

When current flows through a resistance, electrical energy is converted to heat:

Heat Energy = I²Rt

This explains why:

  • High current (I²) causes more heating
  • Poor connections (higher R) get hot
  • Longer time (t) means more heat buildup

📌 Key Points: Dangers of Electricity

  • Electric shock can be fatal at household voltage (230V)
  • Current as low as 50-100 mA can cause heart fibrillation
  • Electrical fires are caused by overloading, short circuits, and poor connections
  • Heating effect: Heat = I²Rt - high current causes rapid heating
  • Short circuit: Live and neutral touch → very low R → very high I
  • Overloading: Too many appliances → total current exceeds cable rating
  • Water + electricity = extreme danger (water conducts)

Electrical Safety Features

🛡️ Protecting People and Property

Several safety features are built into electrical installations to protect against shock and fire hazards.

🔌

Fuse

Protects against excessive current

🔬 How a Fuse Works

  1. A fuse contains a thin wire with a low melting point
  2. It is connected in series with the live wire
  3. When current exceeds the rated value, the wire heats up (I²R heating)
  4. The wire melts and breaks, cutting off the circuit
  5. This happens before cables overheat and cause fire

📊 Common Fuse Ratings (UK/Singapore)

3A

For appliances up to 690W

💡 Lamps, 📻 Radios

5A

For appliances up to 1150W

📺 TV, 💻 Computer

13A

For appliances up to 2990W

🫖 Kettle, ❄️ Aircon

🎮 Interactive: Fuse Selection Calculator

Operating Current: 8.7 A

Recommended Fuse: 13A

📏 Fuse Selection Rule:

Choose a fuse slightly higher than the normal operating current, but as close as possible.

📌 Key Points: Fuses

  • Contains thin wire that melts when current is too high
  • Connected in series with the LIVE wire
  • Must be replaced after "blowing"
  • Choose rating slightly above normal operating current
  • Common ratings: 3A, 5A, 13A
🔘

Circuit Breaker (MCB)

Reusable protection against overcurrent

🔬 How a Circuit Breaker Works

  1. Contains an electromagnetic switch or bimetallic strip
  2. When current exceeds rated value, the mechanism trips
  3. The circuit is automatically disconnected
  4. Can be reset by flipping the switch (no replacement needed)
  5. Responds faster than fuses for large overcurrents

📊 Fuse vs Circuit Breaker

Feature Fuse Circuit Breaker
After tripping Must be replaced Can be reset
Response time Slightly slower Faster
Cost Cheaper initially More expensive but reusable
Convenience Need spare fuses Just flip switch

⚡ Residual Current Circuit Breaker (RCCB)

Also called Earth Leakage Circuit Breaker (ELCB)

  • Detects difference between current in live and neutral wires
  • If current "leaks" (e.g., through a person), it trips immediately
  • Trips at very small current differences (~30mA) - protects against electrocution
  • Required in bathrooms and near water sources in Singapore
🌍

Earthing (Grounding)

Provides a safe path for fault current

🔬 How Earthing Works

  1. The metal casing of an appliance is connected to the earth wire
  2. The earth wire connects to a metal rod/plate buried in the ground
  3. If a fault occurs (live wire touches casing):
  4. Current flows through the low resistance earth path
  5. Large current triggers the fuse to blow
  6. The circuit is disconnected before someone touches the casing
🎮 Interactive: Earthing Protection Demo

Click "Simulate Fault" to see what happens when the live wire touches the metal casing.

📐 Why Earthing Works

The earth wire has very low resistance (much lower than the human body).

Earth wire: ~1 Ω
Human body: ~1000 Ω (dry)

When fault occurs: Current prefers the low resistance path (earth wire)

Large fault current (e.g., 230V/1Ω = 230A) → Fuse blows instantly!

📌 Key Points: Earthing

  • Earth wire connects metal casing to the ground
  • Earth wire has very low resistance
  • If live wire touches casing → current flows to earth → fuse blows
  • Protects user from electric shock
  • Earth wire is colored GREEN-YELLOW (striped)
  • Works together with fuse for complete protection
📦

Double Insulation

No earth wire needed

Double Insulation Symbol

Look for this symbol on appliances - two squares, one inside the other

🔬 How Double Insulation Works

Primary Insulation

Normal insulation on the wires and live parts

Secondary Insulation

Plastic casing that completely encloses all electrical parts

  • Even if primary insulation fails, user cannot touch live parts
  • No metal casing that could become live
  • Earth wire not needed - uses 2-core cable (live + neutral only)

⚠️ Why Switches and Fuses Must Be in the LIVE Wire

The switch and fuse must ALWAYS be in the live wire, not the neutral wire.

Reason: When the switch is open or fuse blown:

  • If in live wire → Appliance is completely disconnected from 230V → SAFE
  • If in neutral wire → Appliance is still connected to 230V → DANGEROUS!

📌 Key Points: 3-Pin Plug

  • Live (Brown): Right pin, carries current to appliance, 230V
  • Neutral (Blue): Left pin, return path, 0V
  • Earth (Green-Yellow): Top pin (longest), safety wire
  • Fuse: Connected in the live wire
  • Switch: Must be in the live wire
  • Earth pin is longest: First in, last out
  • Cable grip prevents wires from pulling loose
