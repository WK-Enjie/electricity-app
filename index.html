Chapter 1 · Current Electricity

⚡ Electric Current

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Define electric current as the rate of flow of charge
  • Use the formula I = Q / t
  • Distinguish conventional current from electron flow
  • Know that an ammeter is connected in series

📖 Definition

Electric current is the rate of flow of electric charge past a point in a circuit.

I = Q / t
ICurrentAmpere (A)
QChargeCoulomb (C)
tTimeSecond (s)

🔑 Key Points

Conventional current flows from + to − terminal (used in circuit analysis).
Electron flow is from − to + (opposite direction).
1 A = 1 coulomb of charge per second.
Ammeter — measures current — connected in series — has very low resistance.

🔋 Potential Difference (P.D.)

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Define potential difference as energy converted per unit charge
  • Use the formula V = W / Q
  • Know that a voltmeter is connected in parallel

📖 Definition

The potential difference between two points is the energy converted from electrical to other forms per unit charge passing between those points.

V = W / Q
VPotential differenceVolt (V)
WWork done / Energy convertedJoule (J)
QChargeCoulomb (C)

🔑 Key Points

1 Volt = 1 joule of energy converted per coulomb of charge.
Voltmeter — measures p.d. — connected in parallel across the component — has very high resistance.
P.D. represents energy conversion: electrical energy → heat, light, kinetic, etc.

🔬 Circuit: How a Voltmeter is Connected

The voltmeter is placed across (in parallel with) the bulb to measure the p.d.

💧 Water Analogy

Think of p.d. like the height difference of a waterfall. Greater height (voltage) means each unit of water (charge) carries more energy. The battery is a pump that lifts water up; the bulb is a waterwheel that converts this energy.

🔌 Electromotive Force (E.M.F.)

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Define electromotive force
  • Distinguish between e.m.f. and p.d.
  • Understand the effect of internal resistance

📖 Definition

The electromotive force (e.m.f.) of a source is the energy converted from other forms to electrical energy per unit charge driven through the source.

ε = W / Q
εElectromotive forceVolt (V)
WEnergy converted to electricalJoule (J)
QChargeCoulomb (C)

⚖️ E.M.F. vs Potential Difference

E.M.F. (ε)
  • Property of the source
  • Energy converted TO electrical per unit charge
  • Chemical → Electrical
  • Measured across source when no current flows (open circuit)
P.D. (V)
  • Measured across a component
  • Energy converted FROM electrical per unit charge
  • Electrical → Heat / Light / etc.
  • Always ≤ e.m.f. when current flows

🔧 Internal Resistance

Every real battery has internal resistance (r). Some energy is lost as heat inside the battery.

ε = Vterminal + I r
Vterminal = ε − I r
When no current flows: Vterminal = ε (no "lost volts")
When current flows: Vterminal < ε

Current I = ε/(R+r) = 1.09 A

Terminal P.D. = ε − Ir = 5.45 V

🔧 Resistance & Ohm's Law

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Define resistance and use R = V / I
  • State and apply Ohm's Law
  • Describe factors affecting resistance of a wire

📖 Resistance

Resistance is the ratio of the p.d. across a component to the current flowing through it. It measures opposition to current flow.

R = V / I
RResistanceOhm (Ω)
VPotential differenceVolt (V)
ICurrentAmpere (A)

📖 Ohm's Law

The current through a conductor is directly proportional to the p.d. across it, provided temperature remains constant.

For an ohmic conductor: the V–I graph is a straight line through the origin → constant R.

📖 Factors Affecting Resistance of a Wire

📏 Length

R ∝ length

Longer → Higher R

⭕ Cross-sectional Area

R ∝ 1 / area

Thicker → Lower R

🧱 Material

Different resistivities

Copper: low ρ

🌡️ Temperature

For metals: ↑ temp → ↑ R

More atomic vibration

📈 Non-Ohmic Conductors

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Sketch & interpret I–V graphs for ohmic conductors, filament lamps, and semiconductor diodes
  • Explain why filament lamps and diodes are non-ohmic

📖 What are Non-Ohmic Conductors?

Non-ohmic conductors do NOT obey Ohm's Law — their resistance changes with current or voltage. The I–V graph is not a straight line.

🔗 Series Circuits

📋 Learning Objectives

  • State current, voltage & resistance rules for series circuits
  • Calculate total resistance and voltage drops

📖 Series Circuit Rules

⚡ Current

Same at every point.

I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃
🔋 Voltage

EMF = sum of p.d.'s across all components.

V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
🔧 Resistance

Total = sum of individual resistances.

RT = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

🔀 Parallel Circuits

📋 Learning Objectives

  • State current, voltage & resistance rules for parallel circuits
  • Calculate combined resistance in parallel

📖 Parallel Circuit Rules

⚡ Current

Total current = sum of branch currents.

I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
🔋 Voltage

P.D. across each branch is the same.

V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
🔧 Resistance
1/RT = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

Combined resistance is always less than the smallest branch resistance.

⚖️ Potential Divider

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Understand the potential divider principle
  • Apply the potential divider formula
  • Explain applications with LDR and thermistor

📖 Potential Divider Principle

Two resistors in series divide the supply voltage in proportion to their resistances.

V₁ = R₁⁄(R₁ + R₂) × Vs
V₂ = R₂⁄(R₁ + R₂) × Vs
V₁ / V₂ = R₁ / R₂
Larger R → Larger share of voltage

📖 Applications: Sensor Circuits

🌡️ Thermistor Circuit

Temp ↑ → Thermistor R ↓ → V across it ↓ → V across fixed R ↑

Uses: temperature sensors, fire alarms

💡 LDR Circuit

Light ↑ → LDR R ↓ → V across it ↓ → V across fixed R ↑

Uses: auto street lights, burglar alarms

💡 Electrical Power

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Define electrical power as the rate of energy conversion
  • Use P = VI and derived formulas P = I²R, P = V²/R

📖 Electrical Power

Power is the rate of energy conversion from electrical energy to other forms.

P = V × I
P = I² R (using V = IR)
P = V² / R (using I = V/R)
PPowerWatt (W)
VPotential differenceVolt (V)
ICurrentAmpere (A)
RResistanceOhm (Ω)

⚡ Electrical Energy & Cost

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Use E = Pt and E = VIt
  • Define the kilowatt-hour (kWh)
  • Calculate the cost of electricity

📖 Electrical Energy

E = P × t
E = V × I × t
EEnergyJoule (J) or kWh
PPowerWatt (W) or kW
tTimeSecond (s) or hour (h)

📖 The Kilowatt-hour (kWh)

1 kWh = energy used by a 1 kW device running for 1 hour
1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J
Energy (kWh) = Power (kW) × Time (h)
Cost ($) = Energy (kWh) × Tariff ($ per kWh)

⚠️ Dangers & Safety of Household Electricity

📋 Learning Objectives

  • Identify dangers: damaged insulation, overloading, damp conditions
  • Explain safety features: fuses, circuit breakers, earthing, double insulation
  • Describe the wiring of a 3-pin plug

⚠️ Dangers of Electricity

🔌
Damaged Insulation

Exposed wires → electric shock or fire. Worn insulation lets current flow through unintended paths (e.g. your body!).

🔥
Overloading

Too many appliances on one socket → excessive current → wires overheat → fire.

💧
Damp Conditions

Water conducts electricity. Wet hands or damp surroundings increase the risk of electric shock.

🛡️ Safety Features

🔧 Fuse

Thin wire that melts when current exceeds its rating → breaks the circuit. Connected in the live wire.

Fuse rating should be slightly above normal operating current.

⚡ Circuit Breaker (MCB/ELCB)

Automatic switch that trips when current is too high. Can be reset (reusable).

🌍 Earthing (Earth Wire)

Connects metal casing to ground. Fault → large current to earth → fuse blows → user safe.

🔒 Double Insulation

Plastic casing = 2 layers of insulation. No earth wire needed — casing cannot become live.

🔌 Switches in Live Wire

Switch must be in the live wire so when turned off, no dangerous voltage reaches the appliance.
